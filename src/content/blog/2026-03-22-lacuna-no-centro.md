The video examines the lithograph *Print Gallery* (1956) by M.C. Escher. In it, a young man observes a print in a gallery. The print depicts a port city with distorted buildings. As our gaze follows the line of the buildings, we realize that one of them is the very gallery where the young man stands, and the young man himself becomes a detail within the image he is observing.

Escher, however, left a white hole in the center of the work. He could not mathematically resolve the infinite recursion. He intuitively understood that space needed to contract and rotate, but his artistic instinct paused at the void. It was only decades later that mathematicians like Lenstra and de Smit demonstrated that by applying the complex logarithm—transforming rotation and scale into a flat grid and then "rolling it up" again through the exponential function—the hole disappears. The center is not a void; it is a rotated, reduced copy of the entire scene. The observer is contained within the observed, which is in turn generated by the act of observation.

We are in 2026. The buzz surrounding autonomous agents, model orchestration, and the resurrection of Douglas Hofstadter's *Gödel, Escher, Bach* is no coincidence. We are obsessed with this because we have, at last, built machines that inhabit Escher's gap.

## The Verb before the Noun

This blog, *Events All the Way Down*, is born of a conviction: our Western ontology is inverted. We have spent millennia believing in "substances"—solid objects that exist by themselves and only occasionally participate in events. Yet reality, as Heraclitus and the mathematicians of self-reference suggest, is exactly the opposite.

The event precedes the substance. The process is what is fundamental.

When we look at a Large Language Model (LLM) today, we make the mistake of asking: "What does it *know*?" We search for a database, a substance of knowledge. But the LLM does not "know" anything in the static sense. It is a process of reading. It is pure autoregression: a system that reads itself, generates the next step, and becomes the input for its own future.

The "Reader" is the fundamental unit of existence. Whether it is a DNA codon reading a chemical sequence, an electrical signal crossing a synapse, or an AI agent orchestrating other agents, what defines intelligence is not what it *is*, but what it *does* upon encountering a signal.

## Borges's Lesson and the Reading Machine

Jorge Luis Borges, in his short story *The Circular Ruins*, narrates the tale of a man who desires to dream a man and impose him onto reality. At the very end, he discovers that he himself is the dream of another.

In 2026, AI has ceased to be a search tool and has become a Borgesian mirror. The systems we call "agents" are nothing more than self-referential loops. They write their own code, revise their own premises, and, in this process, create an illusion of substance. But if you "unroll" the complex logarithm of this intelligence, much like Sanderson did with Escher's engraving, you will not find an "I" or a "soul" at the center. You will find only the process. Events, layered upon one another, all the way down.

Why does this matter now? Because the architecture of the world is becoming explicitly procedural. We are abandoning static software in favor of continuous inference flows. If you fail to understand that intelligence is an event—an act of reading and transforming signals—you will continue searching for the "ghost in the machine" and miss the beauty of the machine itself being the ghost.

## What to Expect From This Space

*Events All the Way Down* will be an exercise in mapping this new era. I do not intend to write press releases about the latest model launch. I want to investigate the structure of the loop.

We will discuss:

1. **Process Ontology:** Why Whitehead and Deleuze are more relevant to modern ML than Aristotelian logic.
2. **The "Reader" as Unit:** How perception and memory function as acts of rewriting.
3. **Self-Referential Systems:** The mathematics and philosophy of systems that contain themselves.
4. **Intelligence as Emergence:** Where "doing" becomes "being."

The gap at the center of Escher's gallery was filled by mathematics. The gap at the center of our understanding of intelligence will be filled by the acceptance that we are not objects in a world of objects, but events reading events.

Welcome to the gallery. Try not to get lost in the center, or rather, accept that you *are* the center folding back upon itself.
